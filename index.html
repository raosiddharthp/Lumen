01 Client Brief

NoordLeven Life & Health — the anchor client.

A composite of real EU life and health insurer patterns. NoordLeven has underwritten personal risk since 1974 from Amsterdam, passporting term life, whole life, critical illness, and income protection cover across nine EU member states under Solvency II freedom of services. An automated straight-through engine handles the roughly 92% of new business applications that present standard risk. The remaining share — a material health history, a hazardous occupation, an unusually high sum assured — falls to manual underwriting, exactly the population the EU AI Act now names directly.

Chief Underwriting Officer Anke Voss is accountable for underwriting quality and consistency across the complex-risk book, and has become, in the last two quarters, the internal owner of Annex III readiness for the pricing and risk-assessment process her team runs — a responsibility that arrived alongside her existing one, not instead of it.

What the brief is, precisely
Not a replacement for the underwriter's judgement, and not an automated issuance engine. Lumen assembles and grounds the evidence a complex case requires, computes a deterministic rating recommendation, and produces a provenance-tagged draft — every case is still reviewed and signed by a human, always. What changes is whether that human is working from a repeatable, auditable process or from memory and three disconnected sources.

03 Problem Statement

Not a shortage of expertise. An absence of a structured trail for how that expertise is applied.

Verhoeven and his colleagues are skilled underwriters. The gap is structural: for a complex case, evidence is assembled from memory and habit — a reinsurer manual referenced because it's the one the underwriter remembers, a precedent pulled from a spreadsheet if anyone thought to log it, a literature check done only when the case feels unusual enough to warrant it. Which sources were actually considered, and why, is rarely recorded in a form anyone could reconstruct later.

Failure 1 — Evidence is habit, not process
Two underwriters handling comparable cases may consult different sources, in a different order, and arrive at different conclusions — and neither case file shows which evidence was weighed or why.
Failure 2 — Divergence is invisible
Without a structured record, NoordLeven cannot measure whether comparable cases are rated consistently across underwriters — a live fairness and data-governance question the moment a system like Lumen is expected to answer it.
Failure 3 — Nothing survives the decision
There is no case-level artefact showing a regulator, a reinsurer auditor, or the applicant themselves exactly which evidence produced a given premium loading, months or years after the fact.
Precise problem statement
NoordLeven has no structured, auditable mechanism connecting the evidence an underwriter considers to the rating decision they reach on a complex case — so consistency across underwriters cannot be measured, and the evidentiary basis for any single decision cannot be reconstructed afterward, at exactly the moment Annex III makes that reconstruction a legal expectation, not a best practice.

05 Regulatory & Operational Exposure

Not a future compliance project. A live one, with a fixed date.

The EU AI Act names this exact activity. Annex III, point 5(c) classifies AI systems used for risk assessment and pricing in relation to natural persons in life and health insurance as high-risk, and the associated obligations — a documented risk management system (Art. 9), data governance over training and reference data (Art. 10), automatic event logging sufficient for post-hoc reconstruction (Art. 12), a transparency duty toward the people affected (Art. 13), meaningful human oversight (Art. 14), and demonstrable accuracy and robustness (Art. 15) — become enforceable on 2 August 2026. Guidance from the EU AI Office has indicated these obligations extend to systems already in production, not only new deployments, which is why this is a live remediation question for NoordLeven's underwriting process now, whether or not Lumen is built. As an insurer, NoordLeven is also within the category of deployer named under Article 27's fundamental rights impact assessment requirement — a governance action item this document flags but does not resolve; that determination sits with Dubois and external counsel, not with this architecture.

The exposure has two distinct components, and they should not be conflated. The compliance component — demonstrating a risk management system, data governance, logging, transparency, oversight, and accuracy for a high-risk system by 2 August 2026 — carries penalty exposure of up to €15M or 3% of worldwide annual turnover for high-risk obligation breaches. The operational component — 4,100 cases a year handled without a repeatable evidence process — is a consistency and quality cost NoordLeven carries regardless of the Act. Lumen addresses both from the same architecture, but they are separately real, and neither should be used to inflate the other in front of a board.
Regulatory Sources
Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (the EU AI Act), Annex III, point 5(c) — life and health insurance risk assessment and pricing, high-risk classification.
Articles 9, 10, 12, 13, 14, 15 — risk management, data governance, record-keeping, transparency, human oversight, accuracy and robustness obligations for high-risk systems.
Article 27 — fundamental rights impact assessment for specified deployer categories, including insurers.
Enforcement date: 2 August 2026, per Article 6(2)/Annex III phased application. A proposed Digital Omnibus simplification package was under legislative discussion as of mid-2026 and had not been adopted; this document plans against the enacted date.

07 Proposed Design

Five gated agents. Each does exactly one job. Nothing is auto-issued — Lumen drafts, a human always decides.

The design deliberately rejects both a single do-everything agent and a sprawling multi-agent platform. Each node below has one responsibility, a defined input/output contract, and a pass/fail gate before the next node runs.

01
Research Agent
LLM · retrieves & cites from two corpora
02
Grounding Gate
LLM, scoring only · pass/fail
03
Rating Engine
Deterministic code, not an LLM
04
Referral Gate
Deterministic policy check
05
Drafting Agent
LLM, provenance-tagged only
Fig. 7.1 — Gated Agent Graph
One deliberate divergence from a standard retry pattern: after two failed grounding attempts, the case hard-stops to a human — it does not deliver a lower-confidence draft.

Review tier — not whether a human decides, but which human

Lumen never auto-issues a policy. The Referral Gate determines review tier, not whether review happens. Tier 1 — the case falls within a pre-validated rating band with established precedent — is reviewed and signed by the assigned senior underwriter alone. Tier 2 — a mandatory-referral condition, any decline, any sum assured above a set limit, or any case that hard-stopped at the Grounding Gate — requires co-sign from Dr. Larsen or Anke Voss. The distinction is who must sign, never whether anyone does.

10 Architecture Decision Records

ADR-LM-01Five single-responsibility gated agents, not one end-to-end agent or a sprawling multi-agent platform
Context: A prior draft of this system considered combining research, precedent lookup, and drafting into fewer, broader agents — or conversely, chaining several existing multi-agent architectures together.
Decision: Each node has exactly one job and a defined input/output contract, gated by pass/fail checks rather than free-form reasoning about what to do next.
Rejected: Both a single do-everything agent (impossible to audit which reasoning step produced which output) and a larger composite platform (adds components with no distinct owner or failure mode for this specific process).
Status: Accepted.
ADR-LM-02Deterministic Rating Engine, never an LLM performing the calculation
Context: Premium loading is a financially material, reproducible calculation over structured risk factors.
Decision: The Rating Engine is Nowak's team's existing, versioned methodology, implemented as deterministic code. The LLM supplies grounded evidence as input and drafts prose from the output — it never computes or adjusts the figure.
Consequence: Directly supports Art. 15's accuracy and robustness requirement — a calculation an LLM could silently get wrong is close to indefensible in a conformity review.
Status: Accepted.
ADR-LM-03Hard-stop to a human after failed grounding, not a lower-confidence draft
Context: A common pattern in comparable research-agent designs delivers the best available output after a retry cap is reached, flagged with a quality warning.
Decision: After two failed grounding attempts, Lumen halts entirely and routes the case to a human with an insufficient-evidence flag — it produces no draft at all.
Rejected: Delivering a flagged, lower-confidence draft — rejected because a regulated underwriting decision that stays ungrounded should not exist in draft form; the risk of it being signed off under time pressure outweighs the convenience of having something to start from.
Status: Accepted.
ADR-LM-04Referral Gate is a deterministic policy list, not a confidence-based decision
Context: Some cases must always reach Dr. Larsen — specific diagnoses, sums assured above a set limit, any decline — regardless of how confident the upstream evidence looks.
Decision: The mandatory-referral list is a maintained policy table, checked independently of the Grounding Gate's confidence score. A high grounding score can never route around it.
Status: Accepted.
ADR-LM-05Provenance-tagged drafting only
Context: A drafted decision letter needs to support both the underwriter's review and, later, an Art. 13 explanation to the applicant.
Decision: Every clause the Drafting Agent produces carries a tag resolving to either a Rating Engine output or a specific Research Agent citation. No sentence exists without one.
Consequence: Slower drafting than free-generation, and a hard constraint the prompt cannot be allowed to drift away from over time — worth periodic audit.
Status: Accepted.
ADR-LM-06One structured log table, not a three-layer audit architecture
Context: At 4,100 cases a year, a full forensic-SIEM-plus-analytics-warehouse-plus-dashboard audit stack is disproportionate.
Decision: One structured event log per agent invocation — case ID, agent name, inputs read, outputs written, confidence, latency — sufficient to reconstruct any case for Art. 12.
Consequence: Revisit if case volume or the number of downstream consumers of the log grows materially past this scale.
Status: Accepted.

11 Objection Handling

Verhoeven — "will this end up deciding cases instead of me?"
The architectural answer is no — every case is drafted, never issued, and a case with insufficient evidence produces nothing rather than a weak draft. Whether that commitment survives contact with case-volume pressure eighteen months from now is a management and incentive question, not one this document can resolve on its own.
Larsen — "can I trust a citation an LLM produced?"
Only if the citation resolves to a real, retrievable source and the Grounding Gate has verified the cited passage actually supports the claim — not merely that a citation-shaped string is present. That verification step has to be built and tested with real failure cases before Larsen's trust is earned, not assumed from the architecture diagram.
Nowak — "is the Rating Engine itself now an 'AI system' under the Act?"
Genuinely open. The Act's definition of an AI system is broader than machine learning and may capture rules-based systems depending on how they're built and deployed — this is a real legal question, not a design one, and it needs a definitive answer from Dubois and external counsel before go-live, not an assumption either way in this document.
de Boer — "another system with an on-call rotation and a maintenance burden"
Fair, and unresolved by architecture alone. Five gated components is smaller than the alternatives considered, but it is not zero — ownership, on-call, and the corpus refresh cadence need an assigned owner before rollout, not after the first incident.

13 Rollout Roadmap

Phase 1Research Agent and Grounding Gate only, term life line. No Rating Engine integration, no drafting — proves evidence assembly and citation accuracy before anything touches a rating decision.
Phase 2Add Rating Engine, Referral Gate, and Drafting Agent for term life. Full case-level record established for Art. 12. FRIA determination completed with Dubois and counsel before this phase begins.
Phase 3Extend to critical illness and income protection lines. Annex IV technical documentation assembled from Phase 1–2 evidence.

Phase 1 deliberately excludes any decision-facing output — it exists to validate that the evidence retrieval and grounding discipline actually works on real cases before any rating or drafting capability is added on top of it.
